Method Heart: How the Texture Scorer Works

This page explains the core scoring logic in paper style. The key idea is to avoid treating texture suitability as a binary yes-or-no classification and instead estimate it as a structured evidence consolidation problem: we combine proposal fragmentation statistics, region-level boundary coherence, texture occupancy, and semantic counter-evidence into one conservative score that is reliable for ranking. In other words, the system is not trying to guess objects; it is trying to measure whether an image naturally supports RWTD-like texture partitioning.

What Problem the Scorer Solves

Many natural images are visually rich but not texture-segmentation-friendly. A highly semantic scene can look complicated while still being a poor candidate for texture-transition analysis, and a seemingly simple scene can contain two or three strong material regimes that are ideal for texture segmentation. The scorer is designed to resolve this mismatch by ranking images according to texture transition structure rather than object saliency. This is why the pipeline explicitly separates positive texture evidence from negative semantic evidence before any final ranking decision is made.

Scoring Principle in One Equation

The final score is the sum of a geometry-dominant backbone plus calibrated multimodal corrections. Conceptually, we start from a base texture-structure estimate and only allow retrieval and language signals to adjust it modestly, so that ranking behavior remains anchored in region geometry and boundary evidence instead of drifting toward semantic shortcuts.

S_final = clamp( S_base + small retrieval correction + small rubric correction - consistency penalties, 0, 100 )

This design choice is central. If secondary signals dominate, the ranking becomes unstable and object-biased; if geometry dominates too strongly with no correction, the ranking keeps too many ambiguous or cluttered scenes. The implemented blend intentionally sits between these extremes.

Stage A: Fragmentation as a Texture Prior

The first evidence source is mask-statistics structure. Images with richer texture transitions tend to produce many mid-to-small coherent proposals instead of a few dominating regions. The scorer therefore measures mask count, largest-region dominance, median proposal size, small-mask prevalence, and area entropy. Taken together, these features act as a weak prior that the image has enough texture micro-structure to be worth further analysis. This stage is deliberately conservative: it is not expected to finalize ranking quality, but it is expected to filter out trivially unsuitable cases and stabilize the downstream distribution.

Importantly, this stage is also robustness-aware: unknown parse cases are not automatically thrown away, because hard dropping on parser noise would bias the pool. Instead, uncertain items can continue to later stages where stronger evidence is available.

Geometry Backbone: Boundary-Coherent Texture Evidence

The heart of the scorer is the geometry backbone. It promotes images with high texture occupancy, coherent region-level boundaries, and plausible two-to-four region decomposition, while penalizing object-dominant area, ambiguous mixed zones, and cluttered over-fragmentation. This stage embodies the central research bias: good texture-ranking candidates should look decomposable by material transition, not by object identity. Because this signal is derived from region geometry rather than class labels, it transfers better to non-semantic texture tasks and stays closer to the RWTD setting.

S_base = weighted(texture-boundary strength, texture occupancy, region coherence) - penalties(object dominance, ambiguity, clutter)

This backbone is what makes the scorer scientifically meaningful for texture studies: it encodes the target structure directly instead of relying on proxies that can overfit semantics.

Retrieval Correction: Keep Rare but Valid Texture Scenes

Stage B introduces text-image retrieval as a controlled correction, not a replacement signal. Positive prompts describe texture transitions and textured materials, while negative prompts represent object-centric or graphic-design scenes. The retrieval score is computed as positive affinity minus weighted negative affinity, then used with a union keep policy that combines threshold passing and per-query top-k retention. This union rule matters because a single global threshold would remove rare but valid texture cases that only match a subset of positive cues. In practical terms, Stage B broadens recall while preserving precision pressure from the geometry backbone.

Rubric Correction: Semantic Veto and Boundary Audit

Stage D adds rubric-based reasoning over shortlisted images. It asks whether a scene is genuinely texture-dominant, whether boundaries are region-level rather than noisy fragments, and whether strong semantic subjects are likely to contaminate texture-centric ranking. The contribution of this stage is intentionally small in numeric weight, but its hard veto logic is important: when the model detects salient semantic subjects or failed overlay-boundary evidence, it can force a conservative downgrade. This avoids a common failure mode where visually impressive images with strong objects are incorrectly promoted as texture exemplars.

Viewed as a system, Stage D is not where ranking is created; it is where ranking is de-risked.

From Continuous Score to Practical Decisions

The score is continuous so users can choose operating points by task. In this hosted review, status labels follow fixed intervals to support quick browsing, while a broader operational cutoff is shown for mining workflows that value recall. This is why the interface can simultaneously present strict selections and a wider recommendation band without inconsistency: they serve different phases of the curation pipeline.

Why This Is Not "Magic"

The apparent gain comes from decomposition. No single component is expected to solve texture ranking alone. Stage A contributes structural prior, geometry backbone contributes most discriminative power, Stage B recovers hard-but-valid cases, and Stage D suppresses semantic leakage. The final behavior emerges from this staged factorization and conservative fusion. That is why the scorer remains interpretable: each major error mode can be traced back to one evidence channel being weak, noisy, or in conflict with others.

Interpretability claim: every final score is backed by separable diagnostic terms, which is what makes the system auditable instead of opaque.
